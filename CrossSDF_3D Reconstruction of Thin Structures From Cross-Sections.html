Abstract

We present CrossSDF, a novel approach for reconstructing a 3D signed-distance field from 2D cross-sections. Reconstructing complex structures from planar cross-sections is a challenging problem, with wide-reaching appplications in medical imaging, manufacturing, and topography. Out-of-the-box point cloud reconstruction methods can often fail due to the data sparsity between slicing planes,while current bespoke methods struggle to reconstruct thin geometric structures and preserve topological continuity. This is important for medical applications where thin vessel structures are present in CT and MRI scans. This paper introduces CrossSDF, a novel approach for extracting a 3D signed distance field from 2D signed distances generated from planar contours. Our approach makes the training of neural SDFs contour-aware by using losses designed for the case where geometry is known within 2D slices. Our results demonstrate a significant improvement over existing methods, effectively reconstructing thin structures and producing accurate 3D models without the interpolation artifacts or over-smoothing of prior approaches.

Method

CrossSDF leverages a novel hash-based neural reconstruction approach that begins by generating 2D signed distance labels from input cross-sections. These labels are used with a novel loss function designed to overcome challenges posed by sparse cross-section data. Our method excels in reconstructing thin structures without the interpolation artifacts or over-smoothing seen in prior approaches.

Adaptive Sampling and Hybrid Encoding

We introduce an adaptive sampling strategy that focuses on regions where thin structures are present, ensuring detailed reconstruction. A hybrid encoding strategy combines hash-grid and Fourier features to capture fine details while maintaining surface smoothness.

Loss Functions and Optimization

Our symmetric difference loss function targets only the regions of error between the predicted and actual geometry, significantly reducing training complexity and improving model accuracy. This method allows for detailed and accurate 3D reconstructions from 2D cross-sections with minimal training data.
